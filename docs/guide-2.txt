To program a visualizer that realistically mimics festival lasers, you must move beyond simple "glowing lines" and simulate the physical mechanics of a Laser Projector and its interaction with the environment.
1. Physics-Based Rendering (The "Look")
Volumetric Scattering: Lasers are only visible in air because of haze or fog. In your engine (Unity, Unreal, or custom GLSL), use volumetric lighting or a Raymarching shader to simulate light scattering through a medium.
Collimation & Divergence: Real lasers have near-zero divergence, meaning the beam stays a tight circle over long distances. Program your "beams" as extremely thin, high-intensity cylinders that do not widen significantly.
Monochromaticity: Use pure, saturated RGB values (e.g., #00FF00 for green) rather than blended colors, as lasers emit light at specific, narrow wavelengths.

2. Scanner Simulation (The "Movement")
At a festival, lasers don't show an entire shape at once; a tiny mirror (galvanometer) moves a single dot so fast it looks like a shape.

Persistence of Vision (POV): Instead of rendering a static mesh, program a "point" that travels along a path (e.g., a circle or fan) at high speed. Use a trailing effect or a decaying buffer to simulate how the human eye perceives the moving dot as a solid line.
Scan Rates (Kpps): Professional lasers are rated in Kpps (Kilo-points per second). In your code, implement a "points-per-frame" limit. If your simulated "scanner" tries to draw a complex shape too fast, it should naturally start to "flicker" or round off sharp corners, just like a real laser.

3. Dynamic Visual Effects
Fans and Tunnels: These are created by oscillating the laser along a single axis (Fan) or in a circle (Tunnel).
Audio-Reactive Modulation: Use Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to map specific audio frequencies to laser parameters:
Bass/Kick: Triggers "Shimmer" or rapid intensity strobing.
Mids/Highs: Controls "Rotation" or "Scale" of the geometric patterns.
Scan-Line "Rolling Shutter": For ultimate realism, add a post-processing effect that mimics how cameras record lasers—creating horizontal bands or broken lines.
